## Q1–Q2 2025 — CFPB Upheaval

*The consumer protection landscape shifts dramatically. The CFPB is curtailed, major rules are reversed or frozen, but the underlying statutes remain. CANVAS must decide what still applies and what has changed.*

### INTEL-ALL-008 — CFPB Operational Curtailment

**Identified**: 2025-02 | **Reviewer**: Compliance Officer | **Review date**: 2026-04-12
**Workflow(s)**: B, C, D, E, H, I, P (all consumer-facing)

**Assessment**: This is the single largest shift in the federal consumer compliance landscape in a decade. CFPB supervision events cut 50%, 70 interpretive rules rescinded, focus shifted to depositories. However, the underlying statutes (TILA, ECOA, FCRA, UDAAP, Reg E) are unchanged — Congress didn't repeal them. State AGs may fill the enforcement gap, creating fragmented compliance expectations. CANVAS workflows that reference CFPB guidance need to distinguish between "guidance withdrawn" and "statutory obligation removed" — they are not the same thing.

**Recommendation**: defer-to-cycle
**Rationale**: The compliance gates in Workflows B, C, D, and H reference specific regulatory obligations. Whether those gates need to change depends on which obligations were guidance-based (now withdrawn) vs. statute-based (still binding). This requires a systematic review under stress — not a quick edit.
**Target cycle**: Next cycle on Workflow B (Lending cluster) or Workflow F (Compliance Examination)

**Disposition**: _Pending approver review_

### INTEL-ALL-010 — Overdraft Rule Repealed via CRA

**Identified**: 2025-04 | **Reviewer**: Compliance Officer | **Review date**: 2026-04-12
**Workflow(s)**: E (Deposit Ops), I (Card Services)

**Assessment**: The overdraft fee cap is dead, and the CRA prohibition means the CFPB can't issue a "substantially similar" rule. But market pressure continues — consumers saved $6B/year from voluntary fee reductions, and 80% of large CUs still charge NSF fees (vs. 35% of banks). The Navy Federal $95M action shows egregious practices still face enforcement. For CANVAS: the fee environment is permissive but the competitive/reputational trend is toward lower fees. This is more of a strategic positioning question than a compliance gate question.

**Recommendation**: monitoring
**Rationale**: No immediate framework change needed — the rule was repealed before it took effect. But the market trend toward fee reduction is real and may affect how Workflow E models fee decisions. Monitor for whether competitive pressure or state-level action forces fee changes.
**Review trigger**: State AG enforcement on overdraft/NSF fees, or a peer CU of similar size eliminates fees and gains measurable member growth

**Disposition**: _Pending approver review_

### INTEL-ALL-009 — Section 1033 Open Banking Rule Frozen

**Identified**: 2025-05 | **Reviewer**: Compliance Officer | **Review date**: 2026-04-12
**Workflow(s)**: E (Deposit Ops), A (Member Onboarding), J (IT Security), P (Financial Wellness)

**Assessment**: The CFPB itself asked the court to vacate Section 1033, calling it beyond statutory authority. A comprehensive reexamination is underway with no clear timeline. CUs under $850M were exempt regardless. For CANVAS: the data-sharing infrastructure, third-party access controls, and member consent workflows that 1033 would have required are on indefinite hold. However, fintech aggregators still screen-scrape, and member expectations for data portability are set by market reality, not regulation. The rule may return in different form.

**Recommendation**: monitoring
**Rationale**: No compliance obligation exists. But the underlying market force (members expect data portability) hasn't stopped. If a revised rule emerges or if screen-scraping incidents create liability exposure, CANVAS will need data-sharing workflows.
**Review trigger**: CFPB publishes revised proposed rule, or a CU faces litigation over screen-scraping-related data breach

## Q3 2025 — NCUA Fair Lending Shift + Fraud Surge

*NCUA reshapes what examiners look at (reputation risk gone, disparate impact gone) while fraud explodes in scale and sophistication. The regulatory floor drops in one area as the threat floor rises in another.*

### INTEL-ALL-006 — NCUA Eliminates Reputation Risk from Supervision

**Identified**: 2025-09 | **Reviewer**: Compliance Officer | **Review date**: 2026-04-12
**Workflow(s)**: F (Compliance Examination), G (Strategic Planning), M (Vendor Management)

**Assessment**: Reputation risk was previously a factor in NCUA examinations — examiners could flag concerns about a CU's business relationships or product offerings based on reputational exposure. That's gone. The proposed rule would also prohibit NCUA from instructing CUs to close accounts based on a person's protected class or political views. For CANVAS: any compliance workflow that references "reputation risk" as an examination factor needs to be updated. This is a real change to the risk taxonomy, not a cosmetic one.

**Recommendation**: incorporate
**Rationale**: Workflow F (Compliance Examination) should remove reputation risk from its exam preparation checklist and risk assessment steps. The Compliance Officer agent card's examination preparation guidance should reflect that reputation risk is no longer an NCUA exam factor. This is a concrete, bounded change.
**Target file**: `strategy/playbooks/workflow-f-compliance-examination.md`, `agents/cu-compliance-officer.md`

**Disposition**: _Pending approver review_

### INTEL-ALL-007 — NCUA Removes Disparate Impact from Fair Lending

**Identified**: 2025-09 | **Reviewer**: Compliance Officer | **Review date**: 2026-04-12
**Workflow(s)**: B (Loan Origination), C (Mortgage), F (Compliance Examination)

**Assessment**: NCUA examiners will no longer review for disparate impact — only for disparate treatment (intentional discrimination). HMDA data analysis continues. This narrows the fair lending examination scope significantly: CUs no longer need to maintain disparate impact risk assessments or analyze policies for unintentional discriminatory effects under NCUA examination. However, the Equal Credit Opportunity Act and Fair Housing Act still exist as statutes. Private litigation under disparate impact theories may continue even if NCUA doesn't examine for it. For CANVAS: lending workflow compliance gates that reference disparate impact analysis should be updated to reflect the current exam scope while noting the continuing statutory risk.

**Recommendation**: incorporate
**Rationale**: Workflow B and C compliance gates should reflect the narrowed exam scope (disparate treatment only). Workflow F exam preparation should remove disparate impact risk assessment from NCUA-specific preparation steps. The change is concrete and bounded. A note should be preserved that the statutory basis for disparate impact claims has not been repealed — only the NCUA examination focus has changed.
**Target file**: `strategy/playbooks/workflow-b-loan-origination.md`, `strategy/playbooks/workflow-c-mortgage-processing.md`, `strategy/playbooks/workflow-f-compliance-examination.md`

**Disposition**: _Pending approver review_

**Assessment**: This is the most operationally urgent force in the entire brief. The existing stress catalog (ST-11 Fraud Wave) doesn't include deepfake/AI as a parameterized vector. CANVAS fraud and onboarding workflows may underestimate the sophistication of current attacks — voice authentication, video KYC, and traditional knowledge-based authentication are all compromised.

A Think Tank cycle targeting Workflow H with ST-11 parameterized for AI/deepfake fraud would be the right vehicle.

**Identified**: 2025 | **Reviewer**: Facilitator → CTO / IT Infrastructure | **Review date**: 2026-04-12
**Workflow(s)**: J (IT Security), M (Vendor Management), A (Member Onboarding), H (Fraud/Dispute)

**Assessment**: Four significant breaches in 2025-2026 affecting 100K+ members each, with the Marquis vendor breach hitting 800K+ members across multiple institutions. The pattern confirms INTEL-ALL-015 (73% of incidents via vendors): the primary attack surface is the vendor ecosystem, not the CU itself. CANVAS Workflow J (IT Security) and Workflow M (Vendor Management) model vendor relationships, but the framework may not adequately reflect that vendor breach response is a more common scenario than direct CU breach response. The 72-hour NCUA reporting requirement adds a time-pressure dimension.

**Recommendation**: defer-to-cycle
**Rationale**: The vendor-mediated breach pattern needs stress testing. A cycle targeting Workflow J or M with ST-07 (Vendor Failure, breach mode) would test whether CANVAS's vendor management and incident response steps handle the cascade: vendor notification → 72-hour NCUA reporting → member notification → fraud monitoring escalation → remediation. Direct incorporation would be premature without understanding where the current framework breaks.
**Target cycle**: Next cycle on Workflow J (IT Security) or Workflow M (Vendor Management) — recommend ST-07 with breach + cascade parameters
